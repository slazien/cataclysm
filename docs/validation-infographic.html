How the Physics Solver Works

Given your car's weight, power, and your tire's grip coefficient, the solver computes the fastest physically possible speed at every point on the track. It uses a forward-backward velocity profile (Kapania et al. 2016) — the same algorithm class used in Formula SAE and professional motorsport simulation (OptimumLap).

Why the progression makes sense

R-compounds (0.913): solver 8.7% faster. These tires offer dramatically more grip but require advanced threshold braking and slip angle management. Amateur drivers exploit only 85-90% of R-compound grip.

Endurance 200TW (1.007): nearly perfect match. This is the most common HPDE tire class, and drivers are well-practiced at exploiting this grip level.

Street tires (1.042): solver slightly conservative. With limited total grip, it's easier to reach the limit — and some street tires outperform their rated mu.

Appendix: Methodology

Solver: Forward-backward velocity profile solver (Kapania et al. 2016). Point-mass model with traction circle, power limit, aero drag, load sensitivity, vertical curvature. Same algorithm class as OptimumLap (Formula SAE industry standard: 1–2% error with known tire data).

Vehicle data: 80+ cars in the database, all parameters from manufacturer specifications (weight, power, torque, CG height, track width, drivetrain, CdA).

Tire data: 45+ curated compounds with calibrated grip coefficients. Categories: Street (mu=0.85), Endurance 200TW (1.00), Super 200TW (1.10), TW-100 (1.20), R-Compound (1.35), Slick (1.50).

Track data: Canonical curvature profiles from averaged GPS laps with LIDAR-derived elevation data. Tracks: Barber Motorsports Park (3,650m), Atlanta Motorsports Park (2,927m), Roebling Road Raceway (3,199m).

Validation data: 33 curated lap times from NASA records, community forums (gr86.org, rennlist.com, mustang6g.com), LapMeta.com, FastestLaps.com, and track-specific databases. All entries include tire compound, modification level, and source URL.
